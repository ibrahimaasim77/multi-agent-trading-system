Both routines independently flagged it as "modest strength, no concrete 24h catalyst" and passed. This is a defensible, rules-following decision — the catalyst-freshness guardrail exists precisely to stop chasing unconfirmed momentum — but it's also the cleanest evidence that the catalyst-freshness bar may be set tighter than the system's own "moderately aggressive" mandate intends. SMCI is a good example of risk discipline working as intended: a real, named analyst-upgrade catalyst, but the stock was already up 13.6% intraday by the time the intraday routine looked at it — past the 12% abort line. It kept running to +15.65% by close, so this technically scores "missed," but chasing a name that already breached the blow-off-top threshold is exactly the kind of trade the guardrails exist to prevent. Call this a defensible loss, not a process failure.

GETY (+84.5%), ACA (+6.6%), and APGE (+46.6%) all had real catalysts and all moved hard, but none were ever in this system's mandate — sub-$15 price, non-index-member, and sub-$20B-biotech blacklist respectively. These inflate the raw "stand-aside correctness" number but shouldn't be read as judgment failures; they're guardrails doing exactly their job. Worth tracking separately from genuine catalyst-judgment calls (AMD/INTC/MU/WDC/GMED/SMCI) going forward so one doesn't drown out the other.
